Snapchat-Inspired Feed

Full-bleed immersive cards with depth gradients, grain textures, and bold typography. Every interaction element is designed for thumb-zone reach.

Navigation Redesign
Header: text-only actions ("Search", "Alerts") replace emoji icons. Clean, professional, scalable to any language. The red dot on "Alerts" uses a subtle CSS pip instead of a garish badge.

Categories: plain text pills with no emoji prefixes. "Trending", "Sports", "Entertainment" — the active state uses a soft purple background + border, not a bright highlight. Scrollable without visual clutter.

Bottom dock: custom SVG line icons (not emoji, not icon fonts) + text labels below. The Home icon is a proper outlined house. Markets uses a bar chart glyph with a badge for new market count. Center FAB uses a clean "+" symbol for quick-predict. Active state shown with a top-edge accent line, not a fill change.
Before vs After: Navigation
Before Emoji icons 🏠 📊 👥 in bottom nav — looks like a children's app. Mixed Unicode rendering across Android versions. No consistency.
After Custom SVG stroke icons with 1.8px line weight. Consistent rendering on every device. Text labels below for clarity. Active state uses a 2px top accent line — borrowed from iOS tab bar conventions.
Before Top header with 🔍 🔔 emoji in circles — feels toy-like, breaks with dark aesthetic.
After Plain text buttons: "Search" and "Alerts". Clean, readable at any size, zero rendering issues. Badge indicator uses a minimal 6px dot.
Visual Design Choices
Full-bleed gradients: Each card's visual area uses a deep directional gradient (not a photo) — loads instantly, zero bandwidth, looks premium on OLED screens.

Film grain overlay: Subtle SVG noise texture on card visuals adds analog warmth — a Snapchat/Instagram aesthetic that Gen Z recognizes unconsciously.

Space Grotesk headings: Geometric sans-serif for questions gives a techy, modern feel. DM Sans for body — round, friendly, highly legible on small screens.

Why Dark Colors Work Better Here

1
OLED battery savings. 80%+ of phones in Nigeria, India, and Kenya are OLED-screen Androids. Pure black pixels are literally OFF — dark UI reduces battery drain by 30-40%. On a $100 phone with a small battery, this is a real functional advantage.
2
Evening usage patterns. Prediction and betting activity peaks between 7-11 PM when users are watching sports, scrolling after work, or following live events. A dark interface doesn't blast their eyes in low-light environments. Light UIs cause physical discomfort at night — users leave faster.
3
Platform precedent in target markets. Every successful betting app in Africa uses dark themes — SportyBet, Bet9ja, BetKing are all dark. Users associate dark UI with the prediction/betting category. A light UI would feel like a different product category (banking, news).
4
Data-color contrast. Green/red for Yes/No, yellow for alerts — all data colors are brighter and more legible against a dark background. On a light background, these same colors look washed out or require darker shades that lose their emotional punch.
5
Gen Z preference signal. Discord, TikTok, Spotify, Instagram (in dark mode) — the apps 18-24 year olds spend the most time in are dark-themed. A dark UI signals "this is for me" to the target demographic. Light themes read as "corporate" or "boomer" to this audience.
6
Perceived premium quality. UX research from Material Design shows dark themes create a perception of premium, immersive experiences. Light themes feel utilitarian. For a product competing against Polymarket's institutional aesthetic, dark + gradients = modern + exciting.

Snapchat Story Mode

The full-screen, zero-chrome experience. No navigation bars, no headers, no clutter — just the prediction, the context, and the action. Exactly how Snapchat trained a generation to consume content.

Snapchat Mechanics Applied
Progress bars at top: Show position in the prediction queue — 5 bars = 5 markets to swipe through. The current bar fills with the accent color. Exactly Snapchat/Instagram Stories behavior.

Tap zones: Tap right side to advance, tap left to go back, tap center to pause and read context. Long-press to expand analysis. These are muscle-memory gestures for anyone under 30.

Full-screen canvas: The entire screen is the prediction. No nav bar eating pixels at the bottom, no header bar at top. The "Close" button is a ghost text button, not a fat icon. Every pixel serves the prediction experience.

"Swipe for next": Subtle animated hint at the bottom. After the first 3 interactions, this disappears — the user has learned the gesture.
Navigation Philosophy
No bottom nav in Story Mode. When you're inside Snapchat stories, there's no tab bar. Same principle here — the story IS the navigation. Swipe/tap moves through predictions. The feed view (previous tab) has the full bottom dock for structured navigation.

"Close" replaces back button. A text button, not an icon. "Close" is universally understood. No ambiguity about what the X icon means (close? delete? cancel?). Text-based affordances are clearer across cultures and literacy levels.

Source header with avatar: "PredictX Sports" with a small branded dot — like Snapchat shows who posted the story. Users know this is curated sports content, not a random market.
The "No" Button Problem
In the feed view, both Yes and No are solid color buttons. But in Story Mode, "No" uses a glass/frosted style instead of solid red. This is intentional — in immersive full-screen contexts, two competing bright buttons create decision paralysis. Making "No" visually lighter (transparent + border) creates a natural hierarchy: Yes feels like the primary action, No feels like the secondary escape. This increases prediction velocity without being manipulative — users who want to say No still can, but the visual weight guides toward engagement.
Multi-Layer Background
Instead of a flat gradient, the story background uses 3 stacked radial gradients — a green glow top-left, a blue glow bottom-right, and a dark vignette. This creates depth and atmosphere, like looking into a space. Each market category gets its own color palette:

Sports = deep green/teal
Entertainment = deep purple/magenta
Politics = warm amber/gold
Tech = blue/cyan
Context Delivery
Below the question, a single line of match context: "Half-time: Arsenal 1 — 0. Saka scored in the 34th minute." This is the key information that helps the user predict — delivered in the space between question and buttons. No separate "details" page needed.
